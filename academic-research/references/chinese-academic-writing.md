# Chinese Academic Writing

## Goal

Use this reference when the user wants Chinese output or when the draft should sound natural in Chinese academic prose rather than like a sentence-by-sentence translation from English.

This file gives the shared baseline for Chinese scholarly prose. After reading it, choose one of two profiles:

- thesis profile for undergraduate, masters, doctoral, proposal, or long-form writing
- journal profile for CSSCI-style, core-journal-style, or article-style writing

## Core Style

Prefer writing that is:

- formal but not inflated
- precise but not overly dense
- paragraph-driven rather than slogan-driven
- evidence-linked rather than assertion-heavy
- logically progressive rather than mechanically enumerated

The desired effect is "clear scholarly Chinese", not bureaucratic wording and not translated English.

## Style Selection

Choose the profile before drafting:

- Use the thesis profile when the task is a dissertation, thesis chapter, long proposal, or other writing that benefits from fuller exposition and slower pacing.
- Use the journal profile when the task is a CSSCI-style paper, Chinese core-journal-style article, or anything that needs tighter positioning and more compressed argument.
- If the user does not specify, infer from the artifact name. Words such as thesis, dissertation, proposal, chapter, or opening report usually indicate thesis profile. Words such as journal article, submission draft, CSSCI, core journal, or publishable paper usually indicate journal profile.
- If the task mixes both, draft the logic with the journal profile and restore only the amount of exposition needed for thesis readability.

## Paragraph Pattern

A reliable Chinese academic paragraph usually works as:

1. topic sentence stating the claim or subpoint
2. supporting explanation or comparison
3. evidence or citation-backed detail
4. closing sentence that narrows, qualifies, or transitions

Do not stack many short sentences that each feel disconnected. Do not make every sentence equally important.

## Useful Chinese Academic Moves

Use moves equivalent to these common Chinese academic functions when they fit the logic:

- introduce a research consensus: "existing studies show", "current literature generally holds", "prior work has mainly examined ..."
- narrow scope: "with respect to ...", "under the context of ...", "from the perspective of ..."
- present contrast: "by contrast", "in comparison", "however", "nevertheless"
- add qualification: "it should be pointed out that", "it is worth noting that", "to some extent"
- derive a gap: "however, current studies still have the following limitations", "discussion remains limited", "the explanation for ... remains insufficient"
- summarize a section: "in sum", "overall", "thus it can be seen"

Use them sparingly and meaningfully. Repetition weakens the prose.

## Common Problems To Avoid

Avoid these Chinese-writing failures:

- direct translation of English sentence order that sounds stiff
- overuse of formulaic sequencing like "first", "second", and "finally" in every paragraph
- empty evaluative language such as saying something is "important" without explaining why
- excessive long noun chains that obscure the actual claim
- repeating the same citation sentence pattern over and over
- mixing casual spoken Chinese with formal scholarly tone
- writing conclusions stronger than the evidence supports

## Sentence-Level Preferences

Prefer:

- one main claim per sentence
- explicit logical relation between adjacent sentences
- verbs that carry analysis, such as "show", "reveal", "test", "explain", "compare", "identify", and "verify" in their natural Chinese scholarly equivalents
- moderated claims when evidence is limited

Prefer revising sentences that:

- exceed reasonable length while containing multiple claims
- hide the subject of the sentence
- rely on vague degree words such as "many", "very", or "rather obvious" without precision
- use imported English terms without defining or anchoring them

## Literature Review In Chinese

For Chinese literature review writing:

- synthesize by theme, method, debate, or limitation instead of listing papers one by one
- place citations where the claim is made, not only at sentence ends by habit
- use wording patterns equivalent to "A focuses on ...", "B instead emphasizes ...", and "further research finds ..."
- after summarizing prior work, explicitly state what remains unresolved

A useful pattern is:

```text
Around [topic], existing studies have mainly developed several lines of argument. One line holds that ...; another suggests that .... Among them, A and B emphasize ..., whereas C points out .... Although the discussion is already substantial, important limitations remain regarding [specific object / mechanism / context].
```

## Introduction And Contribution In Chinese

When writing introductions or proposal sections in Chinese:

- state the practical or theoretical problem early
- explain why the problem matters in the field
- show what existing work has and has not solved
- state the paper's contribution in restrained terms

Prefer contribution phrasing equivalent to:

- "this paper revisits ... from the perspective of ..."
- "the marginal contributions of this paper are mainly reflected in the following aspects"
- "compared with prior work, this paper further ..."

Avoid exaggerated phrasing such as:

- "for the first time completely solves"
- "revolutionarily proposes"
- "fully fills the gap"

unless the evidence truly supports it.

## Chinese Polishing Checklist

Before finalizing Chinese output, check:

- does the paragraph begin with a clear topic sentence
- does every citation support a nearby claim
- are transitions natural rather than formulaic
- are technical terms stable throughout the text
- is the tone academic rather than promotional
- does the conclusion stay within the evidence
