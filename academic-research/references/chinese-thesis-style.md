# Chinese Thesis Style

## Use Case

Use this profile for Chinese undergraduate theses, masters theses, doctoral dissertations, long seminar papers, research proposals, and chapter-length academic writing.

## Overall Character

This style should feel:

- fuller in explanation
- steadier in pacing
- more explicit in section transitions
- more patient in introducing concepts, methods, and context
- suitable for sustained long-form reading

It should not feel loose or repetitive, but it may be more elaborated than journal prose.

## Structural Preference

Prefer writing that:

- clearly introduces the research background before narrowing to the question
- gives enough context for readers who may not already know the niche debate
- explicitly explains why the literature is organized in a certain way
- gives methods, data, or mechanisms fuller exposition
- uses section-level and paragraph-level transitions to guide the reader

## Tone

The tone should be:

- formal
- explanatory
- stable
- moderately cautious

Prefer phrasing that patiently builds the argument instead of trying to sound sharp in every sentence.

## Introduction Style

In Chinese thesis-style writing, introductions usually work best when they:

1. establish the broader background
2. narrow to the concrete problem
3. explain academic or practical significance
4. review what prior work has done
5. identify the gap
6. state the research question, approach, and contribution

Do not rush to the contribution before the problem has been properly framed.

## Section-Level Sentence Bank

Use sentence shapes like these when drafting thesis-style Chinese prose. Adapt them into natural Chinese rather than translating them word for word.

### Background and Problem

- "Against the background of ..., the question of ... has gradually become important."
- "From the perspective of ..., understanding ... is important because ..."
- "Although existing discussion has accumulated meaningful findings, the issue of ... still requires further clarification."

### Literature Review Transition

- "Existing studies can be roughly grouped into the following lines of discussion."
- "Compared with the first line of research, the second line pays more attention to ..."
- "The value of this distinction lies in showing that ..."

### Gap and Research Question

- "Taken together, prior work has not fully explained ..."
- "What remains insufficiently addressed is whether/how ..."
- "Based on the above discussion, this study focuses on the following question: ..."

### Methods and Data

- "To answer this question, this paper adopts ..."
- "The reason for choosing this data or method is that ..."
- "In order to improve interpretability, the variables or steps are defined as follows."

### Results and Discussion

- "The results show that ..."
- "A possible explanation is that ..."
- "This finding is broadly consistent with prior research, but it also suggests ..."

### Limitation and Scope

- "It should be noted that the above conclusion mainly applies to ..."
- "Because of ..., this study still has the following limitations."
- "These limitations do not invalidate the analysis, but they do constrain the scope of interpretation."

## Paragraph Expansion Rules

When expanding a thesis-style paragraph:

- add context before adding assertion
- define the comparison frame before evaluating a difference
- add one layer of explanation after presenting evidence
- close the paragraph by linking it to the next subsection or to the main question

Do not expand by restating the same claim with near-synonyms.

## Literature Review Style

For thesis-style Chinese literature review writing:

- allow slightly fuller transitions between groups of studies
- define grouping criteria explicitly
- explain disagreement, not just difference
- clarify how the review leads to the user's own research question

Prefer paragraph endings that prepare the next subsection.

## Methods and Data Style

When the paper has methods, data, models, or identification strategy:

- explain them with enough detail that a supervisor or reviewer could follow the logic without guessing
- define variables, samples, assumptions, or workflow steps cleanly
- avoid skipping justification just because the method is standard

## Contribution Framing

In thesis-style Chinese writing, contribution statements can be slightly fuller than in journal prose, but they should still remain restrained.

Good contribution framing usually:

- states what the paper adds
- states relative to what prior work it adds it
- states the scope and boundary of the claim

Contribution lists should be ordered from most substantive to most supportive. Put the core intellectual contribution before secondary items such as data organization or contextual supplementation.

## Common Failure Modes

Avoid:

- padding paragraphs with generic significance claims
- repeating the same point in slightly different wording
- using subsection summaries that add no new function
- writing overlong introductions that delay the actual question
- treating "completeness" as an excuse for weak selectivity

## Revision Checklist

Before finalizing thesis-style Chinese prose, check:

- does each section help the reader move from background to question to answer
- are concepts and methods introduced early enough
- are transitions clear without becoming mechanical
- has unnecessary repetition been removed
- does the discussion remain proportional to the evidence

## Thesis-Style Rewrite Heuristics

When revising draft text toward thesis style:

- convert abrupt claims into claim-plus-explanation form
- unpack compressed expert shorthand when a student or supervisor reader may need one more step
- replace overly sharp transitions with steadier argumentative progression
- keep terminology stable across chapters and sections
